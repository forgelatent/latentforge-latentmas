SUGGESTION 1
Technique: Predictive Coding / Hierarchical Error Propagation
Field: Computational Neuroscience (Friston's Free Energy Principle)
Core idea: Instead of transmitting the full latent delta, transmit only the *prediction error* — the difference between what the receiving agent expected and what actually arrived — allowing the receiver to reconstruct the full update from its own generative model.
Why LatentForge: Your current top-k sparsity selects the 128 largest-magnitude dimensions, but it treats every transmission as independent. The brain doesn't do this — cortical layers maintain running predictions of incoming signals and only propagate surprise (prediction error). Applied to LatentForge: Agent B maintains a lightweight auto-regressive model of Agent A's latent trajectory (e.g., a rolling exponential moving average of prior deltas). Agent A only transmits the *residual* between its actual delta and what Agent B's predictor would have guessed. This residual is structurally sparser than the raw delta — high-magnitude values now genuinely encode surprise rather than just activation strength. Combined with your existing top-k, you'd be compressing an already-compressed signal. Crucially, this is *injective* within the prediction model's domain: the receiver can reconstruct the full delta by adding the error back to its local prediction, preserving the divergence signal exactly. The shared seed vector is the natural initialization point for both agents' predictive models, making this architecturally coherent with what you've already built.

SUGGESTION 2
Technique: Genomic Indel Encoding / Reference-Relative Differential Compression
Field: Genomics / Bioinformatics (CRAM format, reference-based genome compression)
Core idea: Encode latent deltas not as absolute vectors but as *edit operations against a shared reference genome* — insertions, deletions, and substitutions — the way CRAM format compresses DNA sequences to <5% of BAM size by storing only differences from a reference assembly.
Why LatentForge: The CRAM insight is that most genomic reads are nearly identical to a known reference; only the variant sites carry information. Your shared seed vector is *exactly* a reference genome analog. Current top-k sparsity discards low-magnitude dimensions entirely, but CRAM-style encoding would instead represent the latent delta as a sparse *edit script*: (dimension index, operation type, magnitude quantized to N bits). The operation type encodes *direction of change* (amplify, suppress, invert relative to seed) as a 2-bit symbol rather than a float32. This reduces per-nonzero-element cost from 32 bits to ~18 bits (14-bit index + 2-bit op + 2-bit quantized magnitude tier). More importantly, the *operation type distribution* becomes a compressed behavioral signal in itself — an agent that's mostly doing "suppress" operations is diverging from seed in a structurally different way than one doing "amplify," and this is detectable by the Shadow Self without full reconstruction. This gives you a governance hook that's cheaper to compute than cosine distance on the full reconstructed vector.
Test in one week: Write a Python encoder that takes a 3072-dim delta vector and the shared seed vector, classifies each non-seed-aligned dimension into {amplify >+σ, suppress <-σ, invert sign, near-zero}, and encodes the top-128 as (index, op_type, 4-tier magnitude). Measure bits-per-element vs. raw fp16 top-k. Test whether op_type distribution alone (a 4-symbol histogram, ~12 bits) predicts cosine divergence from seed better than chance using a small synthetic delta dataset. Pure numpy + Anthropic API for generating test delta distributions.

SUGGESTION 3
Technique: Topological Persistent Homology for Delta Manifold Fingerprinting
Field: Algebraic Topology / Topological Data Analysis (Carlsson, Edelsbrunner)
Core idea: Represent a latent delta not as a vector of coordinates but as a *persistence diagram* — a compact signature of the topological features (connected components, loops, voids) of the point cloud formed by the delta's non-zero activation neighborhood — which is invariant to small perturbations and captures structural shape rather than precise coordinates.
Why LatentForge: Persistent homology gives you a representation that is (a) dramatically lower-dimensional than the raw delta, (b) stable under noise (the stability theorem guarantees small input perturbations produce small diagram perturbations), and (c) encodes *structural divergence* rather than coordinate divergence — exactly what your Shadow Self needs to detect meaningful drift versus noise. The mechanism: treat the top-128 non-zero dimensions of the delta as a 128-point cloud in a local subspace. Compute the 0th and 1st persistence diagrams (birth-death pairs of topological features across filtration scales). The number of significant features is typically 5-20, giving you a ~40-80 float fingerprint that describes the *shape* of the agent's activation pattern. Two agents whose persistence diagrams are close are topologically exploring the same region of solution space, even if their exact coordinates differ — a stronger alignment signal than cosine similarity. Two agents with diverging diagrams are structurally diverging, which is an early warning for the governance layer before cosine distance crosses threshold. This is not lossless, but it's a *governance compression* layer: cheap to compute, meaningful for drift detection, novel as a coordination primitive.
