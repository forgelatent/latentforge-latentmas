SUGGESTION 1
Technique: Spike-Timing Dependent Plasticity (STDP) Delta Encoding
Field: Computational Neuroscience
Core idea: STDP encodes information not in absolute spike values but in the *relative timing between spikes*, achieving extreme compression by representing change as temporal offset rather than magnitude.
Why LatentForge: Your latent deltas are currently encoded as magnitude differences from a shared seed — analogous to rate coding in neuroscience, which is metabolically expensive. STDP suggests an alternative: encode each delta as the *rank-order* of dimension activations relative to the previous state, not their scalar values. Dimensions that "fire first" (change most) carry the signal; the rest are implicit zeros. This converts your top-k sparsity from a magnitude filter into a temporal/ordinal filter, which is provably more information-dense under certain conditions (Thorpe et al., 1996). Critically, the shared seed vector already functions as your "resting potential" — the reference baseline from which timing is measured. The Shadow Self drift monitor maps cleanly onto homeostatic plasticity mechanisms that prevent runaway potentiation.

SUGGESTION 2
Technique: Genomic Copy Number Variation (CNV) Differential Compression
Field: Genomics / Bioinformatics
Core idea: CNV analysis compresses genome-wide data by storing only *structural breakpoints* — positions where copy number changes — rather than the full copy number profile, achieving high compression because most of the genome between breakpoints is flat.
Why LatentForge: Latent delta vectors likely have a similar structure: most dimensions drift slowly or not at all between agent states, with sharp transitions clustered around semantically significant shifts (topic changes, reasoning pivots, emotional valence flips). CNV-style compression says: don't encode the flat regions at all — only store (start_dimension, end_dimension, delta_magnitude) tuples for contiguous "runs" of correlated change. This is run-length encoding, but the genomic insight is richer: CNV tools like DNAcopy use circular binary segmentation (CBS) to find breakpoints *statistically*, not by thresholding. Applied to latent deltas, CBS would find the natural segment boundaries in your vector rather than imposing an arbitrary top-k cutoff. This is domain-adaptive by construction — the segmentation changes based on what the agent is actually doing, not a fixed k.

Apply Pelt or BinSeg change-point detection across the dimension axis of each delta vector. Encode only breakpoints + segment means. Compare bit cost and reconstruction error against your current top-128 sparsity baseline at matched compression ratios.

SUGGESTION 3
Technique: Persistent Homology Fingerprinting for Drift Detection
Field: Topological Data Analysis (TDA)
Core idea: Persistent homology tracks which topological features (connected components, loops, voids) of a point cloud *persist* across scales, producing a compact "barcode" that is provably stable under small perturbations and captures global structure invisible to coordinate-wise methods.
Why LatentForge: Your Shadow Self governance layer needs to distinguish *meaningful* divergence from noise — but L2 distance and cosine similarity are blind to the shape of the trajectory through latent space. Two agents could have identical cosine similarity to the seed yet have traversed completely different topological paths to get there, signaling genuine semantic drift vs. surface-level agreement. Persistent homology barcodes of the agent's latent trajectory (the sequence of hidden states as a point cloud) would give the Shadow Self a *structural fingerprint* — compression of the trajectory's shape rather than its coordinates. Critically, the bottleneck distance between barcodes is stable, so small noise produces small barcode changes while genuine drift produces large ones. This gives you a compressed, geometry-aware divergence signal that current top-k approaches fundamentally cannot capture. The barcode itself is a highly compressed representation — typically tens of bars capture the essential topology of thousands of points.
